Hubble Instrument Overview

Hubble's 6 Instruments

  • The Wide Field Camera 3 is designed to capture detailed images across a wide range of wavelengths, including ultraviolet, visible, and near-infrared light. It is used to find numerous objects, such as distant galaxies, exoplanets and their atmospheres, and supernovae.
  • The Cosmic Origins Spectrograph is used to observe specifically ultraviolet light by dispersing light into components that can be studied. The Cosmic Origins Spectrograph is primarily used to study galaxy evolution, formation of planets, and the environment needed for life to form.
  • The Advanced Camera for Surveys was installed in 2002 and is designed for visible-light observations. It is used to map the distribution of dark matter, studies the evolution of galaxy clusters, and detects the most distant objects in the universe.
  • The Space Telescope Imaging Spectrograph is a combination of a camera and spectrograph. It can detect UV light, visible light, and near-infrared light. It is primarily used to make profiles of planets, including their motion, density, temperature, and chemical composition.
  • The Near Infrared Camera and Multi-Object Spectrometer works in the near-infrared range of the electromagnetic spectrum. It is primarily used to find objects hidden by interstellar dust.
  • The Fine Guidance Sensors operate in the visible light spectrum. Two of the sensors' primary purpose is not to collect data, but rather aid Hubble in navigating to stars. The third, however, measures the distance between stars and their relative motions.
